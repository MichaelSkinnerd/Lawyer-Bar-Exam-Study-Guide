Vice District Law Study Guide

Everything you need to pass the Bar Exam

Basic Legal Words You Need to Know

  • Suspect: A person police believe may be involved in a crime.
  • Defendant: A person formally accused of a crime in court.
  • Prosecution: The side trying to prove the defendant is guilty.
  • Defense: The side protecting the accused person’s rights.
  • Evidence: Information used to prove or disprove something in court.
  • Admissible: Evidence that is allowed to be used in court.
  • Suppressed: Evidence that is not allowed to be used in court.
  • Custody: When a person is not free to leave.
  • Interrogation: Police questioning meant to get answers or a confession.
  • Confession: When a suspect admits to committing a crime.

1. Terry Stops

A Terry Stop is a short temporary detention. It allows police to briefly stop someone when they have reasonable suspicion that criminal activity may be happening.

Reasonable suspicion means the officer has specific facts that suggest something illegal may be going on. It cannot be based only on a guess, a bad feeling, or someone being in a bad area.

Terry v. Ohio (1968)

This case established that officers may briefly stop and detain a person when they have reasonable suspicion that criminal activity is occurring. It does not require full probable cause like an arrest, but the officer must be able to point to specific facts and behavior. In RP, this means a person cannot be stopped just because an officer has a bad feeling; there must be something observable, such as suspicious movement, matching a suspect description, or behavior connected to a crime.

Exam Tip: A Terry Stop is not an arrest. It is a temporary stop based on reasonable suspicion.

2. Reasonable Suspicion vs Probable Cause

These two standards are important because they decide what police are legally allowed to do.

  • Reasonable Suspicion: A lower standard. It allows police to temporarily stop or detain someone.
  • Probable Cause: A higher standard. It allows police to arrest someone or conduct certain searches.

Think of it like this: reasonable suspicion lets police investigate, but probable cause lets police arrest or search.

3. Probable Cause

Probable cause means there are enough facts for a reasonable person to believe that a crime has been committed and that the suspect may be involved.

Examples of probable cause may include seeing a crime happen, finding illegal items during a lawful search, matching a suspect description, or having reliable witness statements.

Beck v. Ohio (1964)

This case made it clear that police must have real facts supporting an arrest, not just suspicion or assumptions. An officer must be able to explain why a reasonable person would believe the suspect committed a crime. In RP, this means officers should be able to describe the evidence, witness statements, observations, or circumstances that created probable cause before making an arrest.

Exam Tip: Arrests require probable cause, not just reasonable suspicion.

4. Miranda Rights

Miranda Rights protect a suspect from being forced to speak against themselves during police questioning.

The basic rights are:

  • The right to remain silent.
  • Anything said can be used in court.
  • The right to an attorney.
  • If the suspect cannot afford an attorney, one may be provided.

Miranda Rights matter most when a suspect is both in custody and being interrogated.

Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

This case created the rule that suspects must be told their rights before being questioned while in custody. If officers question a suspect after arrest or detention without reading these rights, the suspect’s statements may not be usable in court. In RP, this matters most during interviews, confessions, and interrogations. If a suspect is simply being arrested and not questioned, Miranda may not be required yet.

Exam Tip: If a suspect asks for a lawyer or says they want to remain silent, questioning should stop.

5. Searches and Illegal Searches

A search is when police look through a person, vehicle, house, bag, or property for evidence.

A search is usually legal when police have one of these:

  • Consent: The person clearly agrees to the search.
  • Warrant: A judge approves the search.
  • Probable Cause: Police have strong facts suggesting evidence or illegal items are present.

An illegal search happens when police search without proper legal reason.

Mapp v. Ohio (1961)

This case established that illegally obtained evidence cannot be used in court. This is known as the Exclusionary Rule. If police search a person, vehicle, or property without legal justification, the defense can ask the court to suppress the evidence. In RP, this gives lawyers a strong way to challenge cases where officers skipped proper search rules.

Fruit of the Poisonous Tree Doctrine

This doctrine means that evidence discovered because of an illegal search or illegal police action can also be thrown out. If the first action was unlawful, anything gained from that action may be considered contaminated. In RP, one bad stop or illegal search can damage the entire case, including weapons, drugs, confessions, or leads found afterward.

Exam Tip: Evidence found from an illegal search is usually suppressed.

6. Hearsay

Hearsay is second-hand information. It usually means someone is repeating what another person said instead of saying what they personally saw or heard.

Example: “Someone told me Jimmy robbed the store.” That is weaker than: “I saw Jimmy rob the store.”

Hearsay Rule

The hearsay rule limits the use of statements made outside of court when they are offered to prove something is true. For example, “someone told me he did it” is weaker than a witness saying, “I saw him do it.” In RP, lawyers should challenge hearsay because it is not direct evidence and may be unreliable.

Exam Tip: If a witness only heard something from someone else, challenge it as hearsay.

7. Burden of Proof and Reasonable Doubt

Burden of proof means the responsibility to prove the case. In criminal cases, the prosecution has the burden of proof.

The defendant does not have to prove they are innocent. The prosecution must prove guilt.

Beyond a reasonable doubt means the evidence is strong enough that there is no logical or reasonable doubt that the defendant committed the crime.

In re Winship (1970)

This case confirmed that criminal charges must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt. That means the evidence must be strong enough that there is no reasonable explanation other than guilt. In RP, this is one of the most important defense arguments: if the evidence is weak, unclear, missing, or based on assumptions, the lawyer should argue that the prosecution has not met its burden.

Exam Tip: The prosecution proves guilt. The defense does not have to prove innocence.

8. Crime Levels: Felony, Misdemeanor, and Infraction

Crimes are usually separated by severity.

  • Felony: A serious crime. Examples include murder, kidnapping, major weapons charges, or major drug trafficking. Felonies usually carry larger fines and longer jail sentences.
  • Misdemeanor: A less serious crime than a felony. Examples may include simple assault, petty theft, disorderly conduct, or minor resisting. Misdemeanors usually carry shorter jail time and smaller fines.
  • Infraction: A minor violation. Examples include traffic tickets, parking violations, or small city code violations. Infractions usually result in fines, not jail.
Exam Tip: Murder is a felony. Parking tickets and speeding are usually infractions.

9. Suspect Rights

A right is a legal protection that someone has during police contact, arrest, and court proceedings.

Suspects usually have these rights:

  • Right to remain silent: They do not have to answer questions.
  • Right to an attorney: They may ask for legal help.
  • Protection from unlawful search: Police need legal reason to search.
  • Due process: The government must follow fair legal procedures.

Important: A suspect does not have the right to resist arrest. Even if the arrest is questionable, the issue should be handled in court.

Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)

This case established that people accused of crimes have the right to legal counsel, even if they cannot afford one. In RP, this supports the idea that suspects should be allowed to request an attorney before questioning or court proceedings. Lawyers exist to protect the process, not just to help guilty people avoid punishment.

Exam Tip: Right to resist arrest is not a valid suspect right.

10. Running from Police

Running from police can increase suspicion and may justify further police action, especially if officers already had a lawful reason to stop the person.

Running does not automatically prove guilt, but it can make the situation worse for the suspect.

Illinois v. Wardlow (2000)

This case ruled that unprovoked flight from police in a high-crime area can contribute to reasonable suspicion. Running does not automatically prove guilt, but it can give officers a legal reason to investigate further. In RP, this means fleeing from police can escalate a situation, but lawyers can still argue that running alone is not enough for a full conviction.

Exam Tip: Running from a lawful stop can justify escalation.

11. Defense Attorney Role

A defense attorney represents the accused person. Their job is not to decide guilt or innocence. Their job is to protect the client’s rights and make sure the case is fair.

A defense attorney should:

  • Review the evidence.
  • Challenge weak or illegal evidence.
  • Question witnesses.
  • Argue for reduced charges when appropriate.
  • Make sure police followed the law.
Strickland v. Washington (1984)

This case dealt with the right to effective assistance of counsel. It means having a lawyer is not enough; the lawyer must actually represent the client properly. In RP, this means attorneys should review evidence, ask questions, challenge weak charges, and advocate for their client instead of just standing in court silently.

Exam Tip: A defense attorney protects client rights.

12. Police Misconduct

Police misconduct means police acted improperly or violated rights. This can include illegal searches, false arrests, excessive force, improper questioning, or ignoring a suspect’s request for an attorney.

A lawyer can respond to police misconduct by:

  • Filing a motion to suppress evidence.
  • Arguing the arrest was unlawful.
  • Challenging the officer’s credibility.
  • Requesting dismissal or reduced charges.
Weeks v. United States (1914)

This case helped create the idea that illegally obtained evidence should not be used in court. Later cases expanded this rule, but Weeks is important because it helped build the foundation for suppressing evidence after police misconduct. In RP, this gives lawyers a reason to file motions, challenge bad searches, and argue that improper police actions should weaken or dismiss a case.

Exam Tip: Police misconduct should be challenged through motions and evidence arguments, not ignored.

Quick Exam Answer Review

  • Terry Stop: Temporary detention based on reasonable suspicion.
  • Arrest: Requires probable cause.
  • Probable Cause: Reasonable belief a crime occurred.
  • Right to Silence: Questioning should stop when invoked.
  • Miranda Rights: Inform suspects of their rights before custodial interrogation.
  • Illegal Search: Search without consent, warrant, or probable cause.
  • Burden of Proof: Prosecution must prove guilt.
  • Reasonable Doubt: Strong evidence with no logical doubt.
  • Hearsay: Second-hand information.
  • Attorney-Client Privilege: Lawyer/client communication is confidential.
  • Known Drug Area: Location alone is not enough for a valid stop.
  • No Miranda Confession: Confession may be suppressed.
  • Illegal Search Evidence: Evidence likely suppressed.
  • Running from Lawful Stop: Can justify escalation.
  • Felony: Serious crime.
  • Reasonable Suspicion: Allows temporary detention.
  • Not a Suspect Right: Right to resist arrest.
  • Defense Attorney: Protects client rights.
  • Police Misconduct: Challenge evidence and file motions.
